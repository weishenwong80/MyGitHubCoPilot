## This is a overview of ISO 5339, 5338 and 42001
The core distinction: ISO/IEC 5339 (2024) is a decision and stakeholder framework — use it before and during scoping, to work out whether an AI application makes sense, who's exposed to it, and what questions you need answered before committing resources. ISO/IEC 5338 (2023) is a lifecycle execution standard — use it once you've decided to build, to structure how the system actually gets engineered, deployed, and kept under control from inception to retirement. Neither requires certification; they're process frameworks, not product standards.
ISO/IEC 5339 — decide well before you build. It gives you a common language across "make," "use," and "impact" perspectives: producers/developers/data providers on one side, customers/users on another, and the community and regulators bearing the downstream effects. For a GenAI initiative, the value is that it forces a structured first conversation — who is the AI producer versus provider versus customer in your org chart, what data sources feed the model, what non-functional risks (bias, explainability, security, IP, societal impact) apply, and what your regulatory exposure looks like by jurisdiction. It doesn't name generative AI specifically, but the framework is technology-agnostic and applies cleanly. Practically, this is the standard your leadership team should work through before signing a vendor contract or greenlighting a build: it surfaces the questions ("who owns the training data," "what happens when the model is wrong," "what's our risk appetite") that otherwise get skipped in the rush to ship a pilot.
ISO/IEC 5338 — manage the system once it exists. It extends standard software lifecycle processes (ISO/IEC 12207/15288) with AI-specific "particularities": protecting sensitive training data, tracking model drift and staleness in production, accounting for the unpredictability of experimental phases, and recognizing that AI projects need different skills (data science, MLOps) and different risk categories (transparency, unwanted bias, purpose-binding) than conventional software. It covers the full arc — requirements, data readiness, design, verification/validation, deployment, operational monitoring, and change/retirement — as one managed lifecycle rather than a one-off model-training exercise. For GenAI specifically, this is where you'd formalize things like: who signs off before a fine-tuned or prompted model goes to production, how you detect when a foundation model update changes your system's behavior, and how you evidence due diligence for auditors or regulators later.
How to sequence them for your organization: run 5339 at the front end of every new GenAI use case (a lightweight stakeholder-and-risk questionnaire works well as a gate before funding), then hand off into 5338-structured lifecycle processes for anything that gets approved. For ongoing governance across your whole AI portfolio, most organizations pair both with ISO/IEC 42001 (AI management system, the certifiable governance layer) and ISO/IEC 23894 (AI risk management) — 5339 and 5338 tell you what to do, 42001 gives you the management system that makes it auditable and consistent across every AI initiative, not just this one.
For an initial GenAI rollout specifically, the practical starting point is: use 5339's stakeholder questions to scope the first use case and set a risk appetite; use 5338 to define your lifecycle gates (data readiness, validation, deployment approval, drift monitoring) before the first model reaches production; and decide early whether you want 42001 certification as the umbrella, since retrofitting governance after several GenAI systems are live is considerably harder than building it in from case one.
